
# CECL, Market Stress, and Credit Loss Recognition

## Overview
This project analyzes how CECL forward-looking credit loss allowances respond to macroeconomic stress relative to realized credit deterioration using U.S. bank-level panel data.

## Research Question
Do CECL allowance adjustments respond more strongly to macroeconomic expectations than to contemporaneous credit outcomes during periods of market stress?

## Data
- FDIC Call Reports (bank-level balance sheet and allowance data)
- Macroeconomic indicators (rates, unemployment, financial conditions)

## Methodology
- Panel regression framework
- Bank fixed effects
- Macro stress proxies and balance sheet controls
- Robust standard errors

## Key Takeaways
- CECL allowances exhibit heightened sensitivity to macro stress indicators.
- Allowance adjustments may precede realized credit deterioration.

- `code/`: Reproducible Stata workflows
- `outputs/`: Tables and figures generated by models

Study Significance 
Understanding how macroeconomic factors affect CECL estimates is important because these estimates shape how stakeholders interpret bank credit risk. CECL disclosures matter to auditors, who evaluate the reliability of management’s expected credit loss estimates; regulators, who monitor the stability of the banking system and the adequacy of loan loss reserves; and investors, who use these disclosures to assess the credit quality of banks’ loan portfolios. This study contributes to the literature by examining how macroeconomic stress indicators, especially unemployment and financial market conditions, interact with bank-level credit risk indicators such as loan portfolio composition, past-due loans, and nonaccrual loans to influence expected credit loss estimates.  

This issue is crucial because prior literature, such as Beatty and Liao (2011) , show that delays in recognition expected credit losses can make banking outcomes much worse during a recession. So the researches in this study can infer that if CECL is operating as designed, then reserve estimates should build in macro deterioration sooner rather than later, providing a timelier signal of future charge offs/loss exposure. Laeven and Majnoni (2003) found that banks delay provisioning until economic shocks strike, which leads to the procyclical pattern when provisions only increase after credit starts to deteoriate. The delaying of recognizing losses can amplify economic fluctuations by requiring banks to absorb the losses more during recession rather than building during expansions – which in theory should be reserved during expansion too.  
Dataset Overview 
The dataset used in this study was constructed by combining bank-level financial information from bank panel Call Reports (WRDS) (RIAD, RCON, RCOW, RCFD1, RCFD2) and with macroeconomic indicators obtained from the Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED) database (Term spread, NCFI, unemployment, inflation, gdp). 

The fully merged dataset contains 289,000 quarterly bank observations across 4995 U.S. banks beginning in 2020 following CECL implementation, and until end of Q4 2024. After merging the panels and macro data, various financial ratios were derived for later use in regression analysis. Descriptive statistics, correlation analysis, and skewness / kurtosis testing are conducted on the fully merged full sample from the data set. For context, not all bank specific info is reported by all related banks, when these variables are included as controls; STATA will drop observations with unreported data – causing the specific models to drop observations and overall smaller regression samples (~88,000 quarterly observations after controls). CECL Allowances/reserves/estimates are defined using constructed variables from the observable full sample data set and scaled by total loans to construct. Overall, the utilization of full bank panel observations Quarter over Quarter allows the empirical analysis to display cross sectional differences amongst banks and time variation in macroeconomic conditions.  

SECTION 2: Enhanced Literature Review 

Section 2.1 Macroeconomic determinants of bank credit risk 

Prior research demonstrates that macroeconomic conditions are major determinants of bank credit risk, even though the systematic way of in which macroeconomic times of turmoil or enrichment reflect into loan losses varies heavily amongst the gathered studies. Ali and Daly (2010) show that unemployment and macroeconomic indicators significantly affect credit risk across banking systems, in a cross country analysis. Similarly, Louzis, Vouldis, and Metaxas (2012) document that macroeconomic variables such as GDP growth and unemployment are key determinants of nonperforming loans, and the variety of loan portfolios that banks carry, carry bank asset quality sensitvity to varying macro cycles . In futher contrast,  Foos, Norden, and Weber (2010) focused more on bank behavior and decision making. Food, Norden, and Weber found that rapid loan growth during expansionary periods leads to significantly higher future loan losses. In general terms, during good economic times, loan allocations are greater but the banks fail to adjust the loan allocations prior to economic downturns. This suggests that credit risk tied to macroeconomic variables may materialize through the means of  depleting lending standards enacted during times of economic expasion. More recent research confirms that macroeconomic shocks during the COVID-19 pandemic increased systemic banking risk (Duan et al., 2020; Shabir et al., 2023). Duan et al. Focused on the COVID 19 shock , which focused heavily on the important consumer level macroeconomic factos such as unemployment and FCI. All in all, The literature gathered supports the assertion that macroeconomic conditions are a central player in the sport of fielding credit risk goals. It’s also implied via comparison that its not evident exactly whether bank lending decision making or macroeconomic factors play a larger rote than the other, as of now it seems their both primary factors in the determination of credit risk.  

Section 2.2 Loan loss provisioning behavior 

In other literature, the examination of loan loss provisioning and subsequent charge offs begs the question of how exactly banks recognize expected credit losses over time. Beaver and Engel (1996) , pioneers in credit loss research, derived 2 separate components of loan loss provisions: Nondiscretionary and discretionary. Non discretionary was characterized by true underlying credit risk. Discretionary was defined as expected credit loss accounting tied to more judgement and or estimate, by banking management. The partition of two separate components brings up the implication of the capital markets pricing over discretionary and non discretionary components of credit risk, and the differences in how these are trasmitted through the banking system. Later studies that cite Beaver and Engel build on this component derivation, and exclaim that provisioning decision making does not consistently allign with the timeliness of recognition. Laven and Majnoni (2003) prop the notion that banks systematically delay provisioning until their are definite signs of economic breakdowns. The delay in provisioning creates a dynamic of procyclical patterns which typically show that reserves are only changed when credit quality has already broken. Likewise, Bikker and Metzemakers (2005) show that loan loass provisions are heavily tied to macroeconomic cycle fluctuations. Rather than a pure forward looking credit risk assessment based on credit quality, business cycle and general economic conditions play a major role in the estimation process of provisions, which ties to Beaver and Engel’s 2nd discretionary decison making component for loan loss recognition. So from research, its not clear that credit loss recognition is simply based on credit risk or credit quality. To fruther that point, studies also bring up the plausibility of managerial judgement and estimation  in shaping provisional behavior, but a deeper dive into the incentives that drive the managerial discretionary reserve estimation. Bushman and Williams (2012) stand on the argument that discretionary provisioning itself poses the risk of obfuscating bank risk monitoring and the potential outcome of earnings management being a goal. An interpretation of Bushman and Williams evidence can state the polar opposite conclusion, and that the discretionary managerial decision making might clarify lending risk transparency and significanly enhance bank risk taking – but that depends on the incentive of earnigns management or true reserves for potential losses. In synthesis of the related loan loss provisioning literature, loan loss provisions is a permutation of credit risk fundamental analysis, macroeconomic factors, macroeconomic cyclicality, managerial discretion, and managerial judgement. Whether CECL as a framework is a direct proponent of timliness in recognition of expected credit losses is to be determined.  

 Section 2.3 Expected credit loss accounting and CECL 

In relation to the insitutional theory in the aforementioned section of this research paper, a regulatory perspective is taken for the Expected Credit loss accounting and CECL framework design. Post the 2008 financial crisis, regulators of accounting standards and the financial system had inquires regarding the satiability of the prior incurred expected loss model (ALLL). It was theorized that the incurred expected loss model’s fault cascaded into a delay of recognizing actual credit quality depletion and in turn exacerbated financial instability. Beatty and Liao (2011) provide a comprehensive examination into the query of the incurred expected losses model’s delayed effects. It was supported that the delayed recognition of expected credit losses impacted banks lending decision making during economic downturns such as the great financial crisis. The affected decision making during economic turmoil was coined the “capital crunch “ effect which is broadcasted as a disclaimer from banks that their lending will halt after the fact of credit quality depleting. The capital crunch effect gave regulators a motive to rationalize a forward looking loss framework that transpired into modern day CECL. Cohen and Edwards (2017) show that the implemented CECL design mandates bank to embedd forecasts of macroeconomic conditions and forward looking financial information during judgement and estimation of lifetime credit losses. The primary purpose of CECL’s design was to fix the incurred credit loss model’s break points and generally improve the timeliness of loss recognition. More recent research covers the actual post implementation and mid-implementation results of CECL as a standard. Barone et al. (2021) showcase that model validation is paramount and that sensitivty analysis of macro factors must be conducted for accurate estimates. Interestingly, models and sensitivy analysis can vary heavily and Bhat et al. (2019) concludes that the structuring of bank’s credit risk models is a crucial primary factor in determining bank’s lendig decision making and provisoning allocations. Post CECL research underlines the real outcomes tied to CECL’s launch, because now banks adjust their lending allocations and overall credit supply during and before periods of macroeconomic stress – if the stress is projected in the credit risk model (Chen et al., 2024). This corroborating literature for CECL’s design and is a true accounting of credit risk measurement with a higher demand on the accuracy of macroeconomic expecations.   

Section 2.4 Forward-looking provisioning and future losses 

Future loan performance is a possible implication of expected loss accounting frameworks even though prior research is conflicted evidently on the question if provisions are actually recognized in a adequately timely manner. Beaver and Engel (1996) stress that loan loss provisions display the expected future credit losses and in turn capital markets players price the gap in information components of loan loss allowance. This assertion from Beaver and Engel strives to show that provisioning itself as a decision making and allocation of supply can   serve as a barometer of expected loan deterioration. But in conflict, subsequent research nudges that systematic delays are inherent in the actual recognition of expected credit losses. Laeven and Majnoni (2003) demonstrate economic turmoil cancels banks decisions to  post-pone provisioning which is tied to the underlying procyclical pattern touched on in mutlipe other sources of literarure. The procyclical pattern is characterized by the loan loss reserve allocation coinciding with the aftermath of depleted credit quality. Other studies place importance on the point of bank behavior and decision making driving future actual loan losses. Foos, Norden, and Weber (2010) uncovered that time frames of liberal loan growth are trailed by much larger incurred losses in the near future (1-5 years). This trailing of losses effect points to the theory that accumulated credit risk during economic expansions are materialized with a lag, which is a key conducting point of why CECL was designed. The design and implementation of CECL was expected to cover the previous faults of prior Incurred losses models by embedding forward looking macro estimates and borrower and counterparty risk  attributes into the reserve allocation and estimation process. More recent research , post implementation, concurs with the prior assertions of macro and credit risk embeddneess into CECL’s model which draws the subtle implication that CECL estimates are proxies for near or long term loan loss realization. The thesis behind testing whether or not CECL reserves gauge charge offs provides an empirical question of whether the forward looking design of CECL is validated in actuality.       

Section 2.5 Research gap 

Even though prior research shows that macro conditions heavily alter and adjust bank credit risk and loan provisioning decision making, there are still questions pertaining to the evidence to support an assertion of how macro stress truly changes CECL reserve estimates - after the implementation of CECL’s forward facing design.  The majority of existing studies examine provisioning during the prior regime of incurred expected loss frameworks. Other studies typically focus on the cumulative metrics of macro conditions but do not completely investigate how individual macroeconomic indicators modulate CECL reserve estimation. Specifically, there is a low supply of empirical analysis scoped to differentiating the relative sensitivity of CECL reserve estimates to unemployment or financial market stress, denoted by the NFCI. Moreover, there is a limited supply of empirical research centered around if CECL reserve estimates show predictiveness regarding future realized loan losses or charge offs – utilizing bank level panel data after the period of 2020 in which CECL was enacted. The lack of empirical research centered around the aforementioned questions is suprising considering the nature of CECL’s initial design to explicitly include forward looking information into its estimates. This study addresses the gaps by unwrapping how varying macro environments and credit quality indicators inject variation in CECL estimation and by supplemental testing whether CECL reserve ratios expect subsequently charged off losses in the post 2020 CECL time horizon.  

2.6 Literature Synthesis  

Synthesizing the referenced literature, their is not a consensus pertaining to the informational reporting context and timing of loan loss realization or provisioning. Beaver and Engel (1996) show that loan loss allowances encapsulate significant indicators about expected future credit losses. Beaver and Engel also point out that capital markets players price the gaps in the differing components (nondiscretionary and discretionary) depending on the key assumption of if the loss estimates are driven by management’s judgement or true credit quality. But, subsequent research builds on the prior point with a tangent of how provision decision making does not succeed with recognizing the true timeliness of expected credit losses. Laeven and Majnoni (2003), display that Economic downturns are the real trigger that causes adjustment of provisioning, which supports the genreal notion that procycliality is a characteristic of loan loss reserves – which do not move in parallel to real credit quality fluctuations in the past. The prior incurred expected losses model delay of recognition exacerbates financial instability in the past and forced the absorption of credit losses during recessions instead of accumulating reserves during times of growth for Banks internationally. Bushman and Williams (2012) support prior research by furthering that loan loss provisions are tied to the bank’s specific managerial incentives along with the banks specific credit risk fundamentals. Their evidence points to discretionary provisions as a sign of market discpline when the estimation of reserves are tied to the actual signs of depleting credit quality. On the contrary, they also provide the point that discretionary provisioning could show a lack of market discipline by ignoring true credit risk and relying on aggressive risk taking with reserves set out as mechanisms of earnings smoothing. While various points and perspectives were accounted for in this literature review, their is a central theme that directs loan loss accounting, the intention  for reserves to reflect the actual expected credit losses – whcih is obfuscated depending on the process. The process is obfuscated genreally via management’s discretion and or delayed recognition inherent in the process, which muddies the informational value to external users. The welcoming of CECL and it’s inherent forward focused design as an expected loss framework is a counter to issues encountered with prior loss expectation models – which incentivizes the process of earlier anticipation and recognition of faltering credit quality.

This study uses bank-level panel regression analysis to examine how lagged macroeconomic conditions in combination with credit quality inficators influence expected credit loss reserve estimates for US banks . The primary empirical specification is estimated using bank fixed effects panel regression with quarter fixed effects and bank levelclustered standard errors. The fied effects specification is used to analyze how hcanges in macro conditions and oberservable loan portfolios deteroriation are associated directly with CECL estimates over time.  

The regression analysis used for hypothesis testing and to answer the research questions is derived via CECCL reserve ratios rather than directly reported metrics – used to aid comparison across varying bank sizes. For robustness, this empirical study uses logarithmic transformations applied to CECL reserves , charge offs, subperiod models, and slected acddtional control variables for earnings related specifics. Data Description 

 Bank financial variables display credit loss reserves, loan loss provisions, charge-offs, and loan portfolio characteristics, while macroeconomic indicators capture labor market conditions, financial stress, economic growth, inflation, and the term spread. The DVs used in this empirical analysis are derived as ratios scaled by total loans to improve the comparison across banks of both large and small sizes. The main DV for Model 1 is CECL reserve ratio (cecl_total_ratio), and was derived by dividing CECL allowances by total loans. Off balance sheet CECL ratio (cecl_offbs_ratio) is calculated as  all of the off balance sheet credit reserves divided by total loans. The actual subsequent realization of losses is depicted by the charge off ratio (chargeoff_ratio) and is derived by diviging net charge offs by total loans. Due to the nature of skewedness in bank panel level research, robustness tests are performed by applying logarithmic transformations to the primary dependent variable ratios. The primary set of independent variables are the signifficant macrovariables that correspodn to managerial forecasting of credit losses. The IV’s comprised of unemployment rates, National Financial Conditions Index, gdp growth, term spread, and inflation were obtained via FRED and the Federal Reserve Bank of Chicago. All macro variables are lagged by one quarter for the primary model regressions (Model 1 – Model 3) to test the forward looking nature of CECL’s design.  Bank specific control variables are originated by loan portfolio distribution and related credit risk factors – which would also be used in Management’s estimation along with macro forecasting when allocating expected reserves. To characterize loan portfolio distribution, this analysis uses measures such as share commercial and industrial loans (ci_share) and credit card loans (card_share) in proportion to the total loans allocated. This mixed distribution of loan portfolio components poses a view of both larger scale business and consumer credit quality, which is inherently a driver of CECL managerial discretion. More specific credit quality measures are also incuded in this study, with nonaccrual commerical real estate loans and past due loans of 90 plus days. For supplemental robustiness tests, but not primary research quesiton models, P&L indifcators such as net interest income, total interest income, and noninterest income (all scaled by total loans) are included . For the time horizon testing, the macro variables are lagged from 1 to 8 quarters to show the trend in significance in associateion with CECL reserve ratios over  a broader time frame.

Estimation Technique 

The primary 3 empirical models are integrated via bank level panel x-t regressions in stata, with built in bank fixed effects and quarter fixed effects – which is common practice in banking research. Lending strategy, risk management, and managerial policies that play into credit loss recognition are the factors controlled by the bank fixed effects  – moderating the stability of these attributes over time. The quarter fixed effects are applied to the macro variables to ensure that the regressions include econonomic bounces/drops that would systematically affect all banks at the same time, which could be caused by theorized monetary policy changes, inflation, FCI, term spread changes, or unemployment increases. The primary regressions (models 1-9) have clustred standard errors at the bank specific level to limit the empirical data risk of correlation or heteroskedasticity. So overall the variation across quarters for intra-bank credit exposures and macro factors are moderated by the applied fixed effects. A limitation and drawdown is that not all loan portfolio and credit risk measures  (nonaccrual loans, loans past due for more than 90 days) are reported consitentyl across the period of Q1 2020 – Q4 2024, leading to a restricted but adequate sample being used of roughly 88,000 quarterly bank observations spanning 4,995 banks – after all control variables are included.  

Assumption Testing  

Assumption testing was primarily conisiting of diagnostic tests to validate the primary research question regressions. Standard error clustering was paired with variance inflation factor diagnostics, with VIF as a acknowledgement of multicollinearity which can pose data quality risks to macro heavy bank research. VIF tests over the regressions emphasized morederal to high collinearity amongst macro independent variables but many values stayed within common thresholds. To supplement VIF testing, Breusch-Pagan tests were applied to further drill down into heteroskedasticity issues within the regression residuals.

The permutation of the hypotheses results integrates directly into the study’s initial theoretical framework (section 1), emphazing that agency theory is a driver of how managerial discretion is utilized in determing reserve levels. Institutional theory underlies the cruciality of accounting standard setting and active monitoring by fianncial regulatory bodies who oversee the fiscal policty and credit risk taking by banks, which drive reserve estimation reporting. Financial external users rely on CECL for decision making,when allocating capital – the  observation of CECL predicting future losses in near term is useful for decisions.

5.4 Cross-Validation 

Out of sample cross validation techniques are typically applied in predictive modeling frameworks to assess forecasting performance. Because the objective of this study is to estimate structural relationships between macroeconomic conditions and credit loss measures rather than to generate predictive forecasts – cross validation procedures are not applicable in this context. 

5.5 Limitations 
There are also a number of limitations associated with this study. The sample is primarily confined to data from the post-2020 time period, which restricts the ability to compare over longer time periods and across multiple full business cycles. Although including lagged macroeconomic variables reduces concerns related to simultaneity, the study's design is still observational in nature and therefore cannot establish strict causal relationships. Some variables were ultimately excluded from consideration due to their limited availability, which restricted the development of the final model specifications. Additionally, observed charge-offs may be influenced by unobservable characteristics at both the institutional level and loan level in the current dataset.
